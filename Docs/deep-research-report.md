## Comparative Analysis

The most recurring architectural pattern is **guarded multimodality**: text, image, and voice are accepted as input, but actions are rarely left to the model alone. Clinical systems add doctor verification, disclaimers, or escalation. Banking OCR adds confidence thresholds and maker-checker review. HelloKhata adds deterministic lookup, Zod validation, and confirm-before-commit semantics. Diagnostic-centre AI is bounded by doctor override and educational-only messaging. The second recurring pattern is **multilingual local adaptation**ŌĆöespecially English, Bangla, and BanglishŌĆöwhich is treated as a product requirement, not a translation afterthought. The third is **workflow adjacency**: every mature concept couples AI to scheduling, EHR/PACS/LIS, banking cores, POS/backends, dashboards, or admin panels. Pure ŌĆ£chatbot-onlyŌĆØ thinking is notably absent from the most credible proposals. The business context suggests two near-term expansion plays. The main lessons learned from the corpus are consistent. First, AI succeeds when anchored to a workflow, not when detached as a generic assistant. Second, multilingual and multimodal support only becomes reliable with domain lexicons, local-language data, and explicit error-correction loops. Third, regulated or high-stakes domains need progressive autonomy: confidence scoring, override paths, human review, and audit trails. Fourth, integration uncertainty should be designed around early, with phased rollouts and CSV or queue-based fallbacks. Fifth, portfolio materials are strongest when they clearly separate measured impact from target or projected impact; that discipline should be preserved in future versions of the catalog.
